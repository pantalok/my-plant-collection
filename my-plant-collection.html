Texas Growing Season: March through August

Texas summers bring intense heat (often 95–105 °F) and strong sun. Most houseplants grow actively during this period but need protection from direct afternoon sun through windows, more frequent watering as pots dry faster, and extra humidity to combat air conditioning. Move plants away from south- and west-facing windows during peak summer, or use sheer curtains to filter light.

1
Moderate
Strelitzia reginae / S. nicolai

Bird of Paradise

A dramatic tropical plant with large, paddle-shaped leaves that fan out elegantly. Native to South Africa, it can grow 5–6 ft indoors and produces striking orange-and-blue flowers when given enough light. Its bold silhouette makes it a statement piece in any room.

💧
Water
Every 7–10 days
Water thoroughly when the top 2 inches of soil are dry. In peak Texas summer (June–Aug), check every 5–7 days. Reduce slightly in spring. Allow excess water to drain completely; never let it sit in standing water.
☀️
Sunlight
Bright indirect to direct
Thrives in 6+ hours of bright light. Can handle some direct morning sun (east-facing window). In Texas summers, shield from harsh afternoon sun through south/west windows to prevent leaf scorch.
🌡️
Temperature
65–85 °F ideal
Comfortable in typical Texas indoor temps. Keep away from AC vents that blow cold air directly on leaves. Can tolerate up to 90 °F but prefers some relief from extreme heat.
🌱
Extra Care
Feed monthly, mist weekly
Fertilize with balanced liquid fertilizer every 4 weeks from March–August. Wipe leaves with a damp cloth monthly to remove dust. Appreciates humidity — mist leaves or use a pebble tray in AC-cooled rooms.
🏴
Texas Tip
Your Bird of Paradise is on the porch — it will love the warmth from March through May. Once temps consistently hit 95 °F+, consider moving it to a shadier porch spot or indoors near a bright window. Watch for spider mites, which thrive in hot, dry Texas air.
2
Moderate
Citrus spp.

Dwarf Citrus Tree

A compact citrus tree — likely a Meyer lemon or calamondin — staked with a bamboo support. Indoor citrus trees produce fragrant blossoms and small fruits when given enough light. They bring a touch of the orchard indoors and can fruit year-round in the right conditions.

💧
Water
Every 5–7 days
Keep soil consistently moist but not waterlogged. Stick a finger 2 inches in — water when dry at that depth. In Texas summer heat, may need watering every 3–5 days. Good drainage is critical; root rot is the #1 killer of indoor citrus.
☀️
Sunlight
Full sun — 8+ hours
Citrus is a sun-hungry plant. Place by your brightest south-facing window. In Texas, it can go outdoors on a patio from April–September for maximum light. Gradually acclimate it over a week to avoid sunburn on leaves.
🌡️
Temperature
60–90 °F
Loves Texas warmth. Can handle summer heat outdoors. Bring inside if nighttime temps drop below 50 °F (typically late October in Texas). Avoid cold AC drafts indoors.
🌱
Extra Care
Citrus fertilizer, pollinate
Use a citrus-specific fertilizer every 4–6 weeks March–August. Indoors, hand-pollinate flowers with a small brush by dabbing from flower to flower. Watch for scale insects and aphids on the undersides of leaves.
🏴
Texas Tip
Texas is excellent citrus territory. Move this tree outside to a sunny patio from April through September for the best fruit production. It appears to have buds forming — keep the soil evenly moist during flowering and fruiting, as inconsistent watering causes bud and fruit drop.
3
Easy
Pilea peperomioides

Chinese Money Plant

Known for its distinctive round, coin-shaped leaves on delicate stems, this trendy plant has become an Instagram favorite. Originally from southern China, it produces "pups" (baby plants) freely, making it perfect for sharing with friends. Yours looks full and healthy.

💧
Water
Every 7–10 days
Allow the top inch of soil to dry between waterings. Drooping leaves signal thirst. In Texas summer with AC running, indoor air dries out soil faster — check twice a week. Water less if leaves start yellowing (overwatering sign).
☀️
Sunlight
Bright indirect light
Loves bright, indirect light — an east or north-facing window is ideal. Rotate the pot a quarter turn every week so it grows evenly (it leans toward light). Avoid direct Texas afternoon sun, which will scorch the thin leaves.
🌡️
Temperature
60–80 °F
Prefers moderate temps and doesn't like extremes. Keep away from hot windows in summer and cold AC vents. Does well in typical Texas indoor conditions year-round.
🌱
Extra Care
Light feeding, propagate pups
Feed with diluted balanced fertilizer once a month from March–July. Separate baby "pups" that sprout from the soil to share or repot. Clean leaves with a damp cloth occasionally — dust blocks light absorption on those flat coins.
🏴
Texas Tip
Your Pilea is on the kitchen counter by a window — a good spot. In summer, if that window gets harsh afternoon sun, slide it back a foot or add a sheer curtain. The dry AC air in Texas homes can curl leaf edges; a small humidifier nearby or grouping plants together helps.
4
Easy
Epipremnum aureum

Golden Pothos

One of the most forgiving and popular houseplants on the planet. Its heart-shaped, glossy green leaves trail beautifully from shelves or hanging baskets. Nearly indestructible, it purifies air and thrives on neglect — the ideal starter plant for any home.

💧
Water
Every 7–14 days
Very drought-tolerant. Water when the top half of the soil is dry. Wilting leaves bounce back after watering. In Texas summer with AC, every 7–10 days is typical. Better to underwater than overwater — yellow leaves mean too much water.
☀️
Sunlight
Low to bright indirect
Incredibly adaptable — survives in low light but grows faster in bright indirect light. More light brings out the golden variegation. Keep out of direct sun, which bleaches and burns leaves. Can thrive even in interior rooms.
🌡️
Temperature
60–90 °F
Handles a wide temperature range easily. Happy in any Texas indoor setting. Avoid cold drafts below 50 °F and direct hot air from heating vents.
🌱
Extra Care
Trim vines, easy to propagate
Feed lightly every 6–8 weeks during growing season with balanced liquid fertilizer. Trim leggy vines to encourage bushier growth. Propagate cuttings in water — just snip below a node and place in a jar. Roots appear within 1–2 weeks.
🏴
Texas Tip
Pothos is nearly foolproof in Texas homes. Your plant looks lush and healthy. In summer, it will grow fast with the long daylight hours. Consider training the vines to climb a moss pole or trailing from a high shelf. This plant also does great in bathrooms and kitchens where humidity is naturally higher.
5
Easy
Dracaena sanderiana

Lucky Bamboo

Despite its name, this isn't a true bamboo — it's a Dracaena native to Central Africa. Popular in feng shui, different stalk counts carry different meanings (3 = happiness, 5 = health). It can grow in water or soil and is quite low-maintenance. Yours shows some browning leaf tips, which is common and fixable.

💧
Water
Keep roots submerged / soil moist
If in water: change the water every 1–2 weeks and keep roots covered. Use filtered or distilled water — Lucky Bamboo is very sensitive to chlorine and fluoride in Texas tap water, which causes brown tips. If in soil: keep evenly moist.
☀️
Sunlight
Low to moderate indirect
Prefers filtered, indirect light. Direct sun will burn the leaves. Does well in interior rooms, offices, or north-facing windows. In Texas, keep far from south/west windows during summer.
🌡️
Temperature
65–90 °F
Comfortable in typical Texas indoor temps. Avoid placing near AC vents or drafty spots. Can handle warmth but may dry out faster in hot rooms — check water level more often in summer.
🌱
Extra Care
Filtered water is key
Trim brown tips with clean scissors at an angle. Feed very sparingly — one drop of liquid fertilizer per water change is enough. Over-fertilizing turns stalks yellow. If growing in water, rinse pebbles/stones when changing water to prevent algae.
🏴
Texas Tip
The browning leaf tips on yours are likely from chlorine/fluoride in Texas municipal water. Switch to filtered, distilled, or rainwater and you should see improvement on new growth. Texas tap water is often hard and treated heavily — Lucky Bamboo is one of the most sensitive plants to this. Trim the brown tips for a cleaner look.
6
Demanding
Ficus lyrata

Fiddle Leaf Fig

The Instagram darling of the plant world, known for its large, violin-shaped, deeply veined leaves. Native to tropical West Africa, it's a stunning focal point — but has a reputation for being finicky. It dislikes change, drafts, and inconsistent care. Yours has beautiful, large leaves and looks well-established.

💧
Water
Every 7–10 days
Water when the top 2 inches of soil are dry. Water thoroughly until it drains from the bottom, then empty the saucer. Consistency is key — same day each week works well. In Texas summer, may need water every 5–7 days. Brown spots on leaves often signal overwatering.
☀️
Sunlight
Bright indirect light
Needs lots of bright, filtered light — at least 6 hours daily. East-facing windows are ideal. Can tolerate some direct morning sun. In Texas summer, avoid unfiltered afternoon sun through south/west windows. Rotate the pot monthly for even growth.
🌡️
Temperature
65–75 °F ideal
Dislikes temperature swings. Keep away from AC vents, exterior doors, and drafty windows. In Texas, the biggest risk is AC blasting cold air directly on the plant — this causes leaf drop. Maintain consistent temps.
🌱
Extra Care
Don't move it, high humidity
Fiddle Leaf Figs hate being moved — find a good spot and leave it. Feed with a Ficus-specific fertilizer monthly March–August. Clean leaves with a damp cloth to maximize photosynthesis. Humidity of 40–60% is ideal; use a humidifier in AC-dried rooms.
🏴
Texas Tip
The biggest threat to a Fiddle Leaf Fig in Texas is the combination of intense AC indoors and extreme heat outdoors. Never move it outside in Texas summer — the shock will cause massive leaf drop. Keep it in a consistently lit indoor spot, away from vents. If leaf edges are browning, your AC is likely pulling too much moisture from the air — add a humidifier nearby.
7
Easy
Monstera deliciosa

Monstera (Swiss Cheese Plant)

One of the most iconic houseplants, recognized by its huge, glossy leaves with natural holes and splits (fenestrations). Native to Mexican and Central American rainforests, it's a vigorous climber. Yours is a mature specimen with impressive fenestrations — a sign of great care and good light.

💧
Water
Every 7–12 days
Water when the top 2–3 inches of soil are dry. Monsteras like to dry out a bit between waterings. In Texas summer with AC running, every 7–9 days is typical. Yellow leaves = overwatering, brown crispy edges = underwatering. Water deeply and let it drain.
☀️
Sunlight
Bright indirect light
Bright indirect light produces the most fenestrations (splits). Tolerates medium light but grows slower and produces smaller, less-split leaves. Avoid direct Texas afternoon sun — it burns the leaves. A few feet from a bright window is perfect.
🌡️
Temperature
65–85 °F
Very comfortable in Texas indoor temperatures. Grows fastest when warm. Keep away from AC vents. Can be placed outdoors in a shaded patio spot during warm months, but acclimate gradually.
🌱
Extra Care
Support, feed, and clean
Provide a moss pole or trellis — Monsteras are natural climbers and grow larger leaves with support. Feed monthly March–August with balanced fertilizer. Wipe large leaves with a damp cloth to keep them shiny and dust-free. Prune leggy growth to encourage fullness.
🏴
Texas Tip
Your Monstera is thriving — the large, well-split leaves mean it's getting good light. During the Texas growing season (March–August), this plant will push out new leaves rapidly. Each new leaf should be bigger and more fenestrated than the last. Add a moss pole if you haven't already — it will reward you with dramatically larger leaves. Watch for fungus gnats in humid Texas conditions; let the soil dry between waterings to prevent them.
